The current research has established that the 21 cm forest is a "calorimeter" for structure suppression ($M_{cut}$) and that the $dN/d\tau$ shape acts as a discriminant against astrophysical nuisance parameters ($J_{21}, \bar{x}_{HI}$). However, all previous iterations have treated the subhalo contribution as a fixed scaling factor (HAYASHI default). My hypothesis is that DM-baryon scattering does not merely suppress the host halo mass function, but specifically alters the *subhalo-to-host mass ratio* and the spatial distribution of subhalos within host halos due to the modified velocity dispersion of the dark matter. By re-calculating the HAYASHI subhalo boost factor as a function of $M_{cut}$ (rather than assuming a constant boost), we can identify a "subhalo-depletion signature" in the high-$\tau$ tail of the 21 cm forest. This signature is physically distinct from the $J_{21}$ photoheating effect, which primarily affects the diffuse IGM and low-$\tau$ absorbers. Testing this hypothesis will allow us to break the $M_{cut}-J_{21}$ degeneracy more effectively than by using the $dN/d\tau$ shape alone, potentially tightening the constraints on $\sigma_0/m_\chi$ by isolating the specific impact of scattering on the internal structure of minihalos.
