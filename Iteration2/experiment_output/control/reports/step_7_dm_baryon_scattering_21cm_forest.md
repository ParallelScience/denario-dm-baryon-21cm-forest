# Results and Discussion: Optimal Redshift and Optical Depth Sensitivity Mapping for DM-Baryon Scattering in the 21 cm Forest

## 1. Baseline Calibration and the Evolution of the 21 cm Forest Signal

The 21 cm forest provides a unique observational window into the small-scale structure of the universe during the Epoch of Reionization and the Cosmic Dawn. By analyzing the absorption spectra of high-redshift radio-loud sources, the abundance and thermal state of intervening neutral hydrogen minihalos and the diffuse intergalactic medium (IGM) can be traced. In this study, the semianalytic framework HAYASHI is utilized to quantify the sensitivity of the 21 cm forest to dark matter-baryon (DM-baryon) scattering. This interaction manifests through two distinct physical channels: the thermal cooling of baryons ($f_{
m cool}$) due to heat transfer to the colder dark matter fluid, and the kinematic suppression of small-scale structure ($M_{
m cut}$) due to collisional damping in the early universe.

The baseline Cold Dark Matter (CDM) model, defined by no interaction ($f_{
m cool}=1.0$, $M_{
m cut}=0$), demonstrates a strong redshift evolution in the cumulative number of absorbers, $N_{
m abs}(>	au_{
m min}=0.01)$. Assuming a standard halo mass function that includes both host halos and subhalo contributions—where the inclusion of subhalos provides a critical boost to the overall absorption signal—the baseline absorber count decreases monotonically from $N_{
m abs} = 136.44$ at $z=7.0$ to $N_{
m abs} = 13.27$ at $z=15.0$. This decline reflects the hierarchical nature of structure formation, where fewer minihalos have collapsed at higher redshifts. The adiabatic kinetic temperature of the IGM in this baseline scenario ranges from $T_k = 1.16$ K at $z=7.0$ to $T_k = 4.63$ K at $z=15.0$. These baseline values serve as the foundation against which the effects of DM-baryon scattering are measured.

## 2. Thermal Saturation and the Dominance of the Structure Suppression Channel

A central objective of this analysis is to disentangle the effects of the thermal cooling channel and the structure suppression channel on the 21 cm forest observable. The quantitative evaluation reveals a stark dichotomy between the efficacies of these two mechanisms, fundamentally shaping the observational strategy for detecting DM-baryon interactions.

### 2.1. Inefficacy of the Baryon Cooling Channel

The thermal channel is parameterized via the cooling fraction $f_{
m cool} = T_k / T_k^{
m ad}$, representing the ratio of the modified IGM temperature to the adiabatic baseline. Remarkably, the grid analysis demonstrates that the 21 cm forest is almost entirely insensitive to further baryon cooling. At $z=10$, reducing $f_{
m cool}$ from 1.0 (the CDM baseline) down to 0.02 (representing extreme cooling) yields absolutely no measurable change in the total absorber count, which remains constant at $N_{
m abs} = 58.98$ across all evaluated values. Across the entire redshift range from $z=7$ to $z=15$, the maximum fractional change induced by the cooling channel is a negligible $\Delta N_{
m abs}/N_{
m abs} \approx 0.18\%$ at $z=12$.

This insensitivity can be understood through the fundamental physics of the 21 cm transition. The optical depth $\tau$ of an absorber is inversely proportional to the spin temperature $T_S$. In the dense environments of minihalos, atomic collisions efficiently couple the spin temperature to the kinetic temperature of the gas ($T_S \approx T_k$). However, the adiabatic IGM temperature at these redshifts is already extremely low, ranging from approximately 1 to 5 K. At such ultra-cold temperatures, the 21 cm transition approaches thermal saturation; the vast majority of neutral hydrogen atoms are already in the lower hyperfine ground state. Consequently, further extracting heat from the baryons via dark matter interactions (for instance, lowering $T_k$ to $\sim 0.04$ K) does not significantly increase the population of the lower state, and the optical depth remains largely unchanged. The thermal channel is thus rendered observationally inert in the context of the 21 cm forest.

### 2.2. Dominance of the Structure Suppression Channel

In stark contrast to the thermal channel, the structure suppression channel, parameterized by the half-mode mass cutoff $M_{
m cut}$, exerts a profound and easily detectable influence on the 21 cm forest. Because the forest signal is predominantly generated by low-mass minihalos (typically in the range of $10^4 - 10^7 \, M_\odot$), any truncation of the matter power spectrum at these scales directly removes the primary sources of absorption.

At $z=10$, introducing a mild cutoff of $M_{
m cut} = 10^4 \, M_\odot$ reduces the absorber count from 58.98 to 31.25, representing a massive 47.0% suppression. Stronger scattering models yield even more dramatic deficits: $M_{
m cut} = 10^5 \, M_\odot$ results in an 82.1% suppression ($N_{
m abs} = 10.56$), and $M_{
m cut} = 10^6 \, M_\odot$ eradicates nearly all absorbers, yielding a 94.5% suppression ($N_{
m abs} = 3.22$). This behavior is visually confirmed in the N(>tau) suppression figure, which illustrates the severe deformation and downward shift of the cumulative optical depth distribution under varying $M_{
m cut}$, juxtaposed against the completely static distribution under varying $f_{
m cool}$.

The redshift dependence of this suppression is also notable. The fractional change $\Delta N_{
m abs}/N_{
m abs}$ for a given $M_{
m cut}$ becomes more pronounced at higher redshifts. For example, a cutoff of $M_{
m cut} = 10^5 \, M_\odot$ induces a 79.7% suppression at $z=7$, which increases to an 86.3% suppression at $z=15$. This trend occurs because structure formation is less advanced at higher redshifts; the halo mass function is steeper, and a larger fraction of the total collapsed mass resides in halos near the cutoff scale.

It is therefore concluded that the 21 cm forest is uniquely and overwhelmingly sensitive to the structure suppression channel of DM-baryon scattering. This finding justifies focusing all subsequent statistical forecasts entirely on the $M_{
m cut}$ parameter, as the thermal channel provides no viable signal.

## 3. Fisher Forecast and Astrophysical Degeneracies

To quantify the observational viability of detecting this structure suppression with next-generation facilities like the Square Kilometre Array (SKA), a Fisher matrix analysis was performed on the differential absorber distribution $dN/d\tau$. The analysis assumes a baseline observational campaign comprising 10 high-redshift radio-loud sightlines, subject to Poisson noise.

### 3.1. Unmarginalized Constraints

If the astrophysical state of the IGM were perfectly known, the 21 cm forest would provide exquisite constraints on DM-baryon scattering. The unmarginalized 1-$\sigma$ uncertainty on $M_{
m cut}$ is remarkably tight, ranging from $\sigma(M_{
m cut}) \approx 504 \, M_\odot$ at $z=7$ to $1920 \, M_\odot$ at $z=15$. The degradation of the unmarginalized sensitivity at higher redshifts is primarily driven by the declining baseline number of absorbers, which inherently increases the Poisson noise of the measurement. In this idealized scenario, even minute deviations from the CDM matter power spectrum would be readily detectable.

### 3.2. The Impact of Astrophysical Nuisance Parameters

In reality, the 21 cm forest signal is heavily modulated by astrophysical "nuisance parameters," most notably the intensity of the ionizing ultraviolet background ($J_{21}$) and the global neutral hydrogen fraction ($\bar{x}_{\rm HI}$). The UV background is particularly problematic because it induces Jeans filtering—photoheating the IGM gas, raising its pressure, and preventing it from accreting into shallow minihalo potential wells. This astrophysical suppression of low-mass halos is highly degenerate with the cosmological suppression induced by DM-baryon scattering ($M_{
m cut}$).

When marginalizing over $J_{21}$ and $\bar{x}_{\rm HI}$, the constraining power of the 21 cm forest is significantly degraded. The marginalized uncertainty $\sigma(M_{
m cut})$ becomes remarkably flat across the redshift range, hovering consistently around $1.58 \times 10^4 \, M_\odot$.

The degradation factor—defined as the ratio of the marginalized to unmarginalized uncertainty—exhibits a strong redshift dependence, as illustrated in the sigma(M_cut) vs. z figure. At $z=7$, the degradation is severe, reaching a factor of 31.4x. This reflects the strong degeneracy between $M_{
m cut}$ and the rising UV background during the late stages of Reionization, where Jeans filtering is most prominent. At higher redshifts, where the UV background is weaker and the universe is more neutral, the degeneracy relaxes; by $z=15$, the degradation factor drops to 8.3x.

### 3.3. Identifying the Optimal Observational Window

The interplay between the raw signal strength (which favors lower redshifts due to higher $N_{
m abs}$) and the astrophysical degradation (which favors higher redshifts due to weaker Jeans filtering) defines an optimal observational window. The Fisher analysis identifies $z \sim 10$ as the optimal redshift for constraining DM-baryon scattering. At $z=10$, the baseline absorber count is sufficiently robust ($N_{
m abs} \approx 59$) to suppress Poisson noise, while the degradation factor is moderate (19.5x). The resulting marginalized constraint at this optimal redshift is $\sigma(M_{
m cut}) = 1.58 \times 10^4 \, M_\odot$. 

Furthermore, an analysis of the Fisher information content per $\tau$-bin indicates that observations should prioritize the intermediate optical depth regime ($\tau \sim 0.01 - 0.1$). This specific range is most sensitive to the truncation of the halo mass function at $10^4 - 10^5 \, M_\odot$, maximizing the signal-to-noise ratio for detecting $M_{
m cut}$ while minimizing the impact of noise from very weak or saturated absorption lines.

## 4. Cross-Section Sensitivity and Exclusion Contours

To place these results in the broader context of dark matter direct detection, the marginalized $M_{
m cut}$ constraints were mapped to the fundamental DM-baryon scattering cross-section, parameterized as $\sigma(v) = \sigma_0 v^n$. Both velocity-independent ($n=0$) and Coulomb-like ($n=-4$) interaction models were evaluated.

### 4.1. Velocity-Independent Scattering ($n=0$)

For a velocity-independent cross-section, the mapping from the cutoff mass is approximately $\sigma_0/m_\chi \approx 10^{-29} \times M_{
m cut} \, [\rm cm^2/GeV]$. Applying the marginalized constraints, a 1-$\sigma$ sensitivity of $\sigma_0/m_\chi \approx 1.58 \times 10^{-25} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$ and a 2-$\sigma$ sensitivity of $3.17 \times 10^{-25} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$ are forecasted at $z=10$. Because the marginalized $M_{
m cut}$ constraint is relatively flat across $z=7-15$, this cross-section sensitivity remains highly stable across the entire redshift range.

As shown in the exclusion contour figure, this forecast represents a monumental improvement over existing cosmological bounds. The 21 cm forest 1-$\sigma$ sensitivity is approximately three orders of magnitude more stringent than current Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) limits ($\sim 10^{-22} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$) and four orders of magnitude stronger than limits derived from the global 21 cm signal by EDGES and SARAS3 ($\sim 10^{-21} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$). This demonstrates the unparalleled power of small-scale structure probes for constraining velocity-independent interactions.

### 4.2. Coulomb-like Scattering ($n=-4$)

For a Coulomb-like interaction, the scattering rate is heavily weighted by the relative velocity between the dark matter and baryon fluids, which decays as $(1+z)$ as the universe expands. Consequently, the mapping function incorporates a strong redshift dependence: $\sigma_0/m_\chi \approx 10^{-45} \times M_{
m cut} \times ((1+z)/11)^{-4} \, [\rm cm^2/GeV]$. 

Due to this velocity scaling, the sensitivity of the 21 cm forest to $n=-4$ models improves significantly at higher redshifts. The 1-$\sigma$ sensitivity ranges from $5.68 \times 10^{-41} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$ at $z=7$ down to an exquisite $3.56 \times 10^{-42} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$ at $z=15$. At the identified optimal redshift of $z=10$, the 1-$\sigma$ limit is $1.58 \times 10^{-41} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$, with a 2-$\sigma$ limit of $3.17 \times 10^{-41} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$. 

The exclusion contour figure demonstrates that for $n=-4$, the 21 cm forest forecast is highly competitive with, and slightly surpasses, the stringent limits set by the CMB ($\sim 10^{-41} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$) and the global 21 cm signal ($\sim 10^{-40} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$). The ability to probe such small cross-sections highlights the efficacy of the 21 cm forest as a complementary tool for investigating velocity-dependent dark matter models.

## 5. Discussion: The Unique Complementarity of the 21 cm Forest

The results of this semianalytic study establish the 21 cm forest as a premier, highly sensitive probe of dark matter-baryon scattering, distinct from and complementary to existing cosmological observables. 

The global 21 cm signal and the CMB are primarily sensitive to the *thermal* history of the universe. They constrain DM-baryon scattering by searching for anomalous cooling of the IGM. However, as demonstrated in Section 2.1, the thermal channel suffers from saturation in the cold IGM limit. Global signal constraints therefore rely heavily on the precise timing of the X-ray heating epoch; if X-ray heating occurs early, the IGM warms up, and the thermal signature of DM scattering is erased, severely weakening the constraints.

In contrast, the 21 cm forest is uniquely sensitive to the *kinematic* suppression of small-scale structure. The collisional damping induced by DM-baryon scattering in the early universe leaves a permanent, cumulative imprint on the halo mass function. Even if the IGM is subsequently heated by astrophysical X-rays, the minihalos that failed to form due to the $M_{
m cut}$ truncation cannot be resurrected. This makes the structure suppression channel a much cleaner and more robust probe of dark matter physics, provided that the astrophysical degeneracies associated with UV Jeans filtering can be adequately modeled or marginalized out.

The Fisher forecasts indicate that even after severe marginalization over astrophysical uncertainties, the 21 cm forest can probe velocity-independent cross-sections down to $\sim 10^{-25} \, \rm cm^2/GeV$, exploring parameter space that is entirely inaccessible to the CMB or the global 21 cm signal. To realize this potential in the SKA era, observational strategies must be optimized. It is recommended to prioritize deep integrations of radio-loud background sources at $z \sim 10$, focusing analytical efforts on the intermediate optical depth regime ($\tau \sim 0.01 - 0.1$). Furthermore, developing robust cross-correlation techniques with other EoR observables will be crucial to independently constrain the ionizing background and break the $M_{
m cut}-J_{21}$ degeneracy, thereby unlocking the full unmarginalized potential of the 21 cm forest.
