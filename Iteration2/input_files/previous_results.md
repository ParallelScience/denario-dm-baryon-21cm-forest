# Results and Discussion

## 1. Dominance of Structure Suppression over Thermal Cooling

The 21 cm forest is sensitive to dark matter-baryon scattering through two distinct physical channels: the thermal cooling of the intergalactic medium (IGM) due to heat transfer to the colder dark matter fluid, and the suppression of small-scale structure due to collisional damping. A primary objective of this study is to quantitatively disentangle these two effects and determine the dominant driver of the 21 cm forest signal modifications.

The semianalytic computations utilizing the HAYASHI framework reveal a stark dichotomy in the sensitivity of the 21 cm forest to these two channels. Under the standard Cold Dark Matter (CDM) baseline ($f_{cool}=1.0, M_{cut}=0$), the cumulative number of absorbers per line of sight ($N_{abs}$ for $\tau > 0.01$) exhibits a strong redshift evolution, declining from $N_{abs} \approx 136.4$ at $z=7$ to $N_{abs} \approx 13.3$ at $z=15$. This baseline reflects the hierarchical buildup of minihalos during the Epoch of Reionization.

When introducing the thermal cooling channel (parameterized by the cooling fraction $f_{cool} = T_k / T_k^{ad}$), the impact on the absorber statistics is found to be entirely negligible. Across the entire redshift range $z \in [7, 15]$, reducing the IGM kinetic temperature to as low as 2% of its adiabatic value ($f_{cool}=0.02$) yields a maximum fractional change in $N_{abs}$ of merely $\sim 0.18\%$ (observed at $z=12$). This extreme insensitivity arises from the thermodynamic state of the high-redshift IGM. In the adiabatic limit, the IGM kinetic temperature is already exceptionally low, ranging from $T_k \approx 1.16$ K at $z=7$ to $T_k \approx 4.63$ K at $z=15$. Because the 21 cm optical depth $\tau$ is inversely proportional to the spin temperature $T_s$, and $T_s$ is strongly coupled to $T_k$ in the dense environments of minihalos, one might expect cooling to increase $\tau$. However, in this cold limit, the spin temperature coupling saturates. Furthermore, the 21 cm forest observable relies on counting discrete absorption lines above a specific optical depth threshold ($\tau > 0.01$). The minihalos responsible for these lines are already sufficiently cold and dense; further cooling the diffuse IGM or the halo outskirts does not create new halos, nor does it push a statistically significant number of sub-threshold absorbers across the $\tau = 0.01$ boundary.

In stark contrast, the structure suppression channel (parameterized by the half-mode mass cutoff $M_{cut}$) dominates the signal modification. The introduction of a cutoff mass drastically reduces the abundance of minihalos, leading to a severe depletion in the number of observed absorption lines. At $z=7$, an effective cutoff of $M_{cut} = 10^4 M_\odot$ results in a $50.2\%$ reduction in $N_{abs}$. A more aggressive cutoff of $M_{cut} = 10^5 M_\odot$ eliminates $79.7\%$ of the absorbers, and $M_{cut} = 10^6 M_\odot$ removes $92.5\%$. This fractional sensitivity is visualized in the generated heatmap (<code>step_2_fractional_sensitivity_heatmap_1_20260413_113207.png</code>), which unequivocally demonstrates that the 21 cm forest is a direct probe of the small-scale matter power spectrum.

These findings highlight the unique complementarity of the 21 cm forest to the global 21 cm signal. While the global signal (a sky-averaged quantity) is highly sensitive to the thermal history of the universe and thus excels at probing the cooling channel, it washes out small-scale spatial information. The 21 cm forest, conversely, is virtually blind to the thermal channel at these redshifts but serves as an exceptionally precise calorimeter for small-scale structure suppression.

## 2. Spectral Shape Analysis and Parameter Degeneracy

While the total absorber count $N_{abs}$ provides a strong integrated metric for structure suppression, relying solely on total counts introduces potential degeneracies with astrophysical nuisance parameters and redshift evolution. To quantify this, a shape similarity analysis was performed on the differential optical depth distributions, $dN/d\tau$.

By computing the cosine similarity between the normalized $dN/d\tau$ vectors across different redshifts and $M_{cut}$ values, we observe a distinct degeneracy between the suppression of structure via dark matter interactions and the natural hierarchical absence of structure at higher redshifts. For instance, the spectral shape of the absorber distribution at $z=7$ with a strong suppression of $M_{cut} = 10^5 M_\odot$ exhibits a high cosine similarity ($>0.97$) with the pure CDM baseline at $z=15$. Physically, this indicates that a universe at $z=7$ where small halos have been erased by dark matter-baryon scattering looks remarkably similar—in terms of the relative distribution of weak versus strong absorbers—to a pristine CDM universe at $z=15$ where those small halos have simply not yet had time to form.

However, this degeneracy is broken when considering the absolute amplitude of the signal and the precise $\tau$-dependence over a broad dynamic range. The differential distribution $dN/d\tau$ for $M_{cut} > 0$ exhibits a characteristic steepening at low optical depths compared to the CDM baseline. Because low-$\tau$ absorbers correspond predominantly to lower-mass minihalos, the $M_{cut}$ transfer function preferentially depletes the low-$\tau$ end of the spectrum while leaving the high-$\tau$ tail (dominated by more massive, resilient halos) relatively unperturbed. This spectral tilting is the fundamental signature that allows for the robust extraction of dark matter parameters, even in the presence of unknown overall normalizations.

## 3. Fisher Forecasts for SKA-Era Sensitivity

To rigorously quantify the detectability of the structure suppression signature, a Fisher matrix forecast was conducted tailored to the capabilities of the upcoming Square Kilometre Array (SKA). The analysis utilized the differential distribution $dN/d\tau$ and assumed a Poisson noise model, $\sigma(dN/d\tau) = \sqrt{N_{obs}} / \Delta\tau$. Crucially, to ensure the robustness of the forecast against astrophysical uncertainties (such as the normalization of the halo mass function, the fraction of gas retained in minihalos, or fluctuations in the ionizing background), the Fisher matrix marginalized over an unknown overall amplitude parameter $A$.

Assuming a fiducial observational campaign yielding $N_{sightlines} = 10$ high-redshift background radio sources, the 21 cm forest provides stringent 1-$\sigma$ constraints on the cutoff mass. The marginalized constraints are:
* $z = 7$: $\sigma(M_{cut}) \approx 1317 M_\odot$
* $z = 8$: $\sigma(M_{cut}) \approx 1428 M_\odot$
* $z = 10$: $\sigma(M_{cut}) \approx 1807 M_\odot$
* $z = 15$: $\sigma(M_{cut}) \approx 4177 M_\odot$

The degradation of absolute sensitivity at higher redshifts is driven by the lower baseline number of absorbers, which increases the Poisson noise. It is noteworthy that the unmarginalized constraint at $z=7$ is significantly tighter ($\sim 504 M_\odot$). The factor of $\sim 2.6$ increase in the error margin upon marginalization underscores the importance of the spectral shape ($dN/d\tau$) over the absolute amplitude. The fact that a $\sim 1300 M_\odot$ constraint can be achieved relying purely on the shape of the optical depth distribution demonstrates the immense statistical power of the 21 cm forest.

Furthermore, the sensitivity scales favorably with the number of observed sightlines. As illustrated in the generated forecast plot (<code>step_3_fisher_mcut_vs_sightlines_3_20260413_113526.png</code>), expanding the observational campaign to $N_{sightlines} = 100$ improves the 1-$\sigma$ constraint at $z=7$ to an unprecedented $\sigma(M_{cut}) \approx 416 M_\odot$. This level of sensitivity probes the very threshold of minihalo formation, pushing into regimes previously considered inaccessible.

## 4. Constraints on Dark Matter-Baryon Scattering Cross-Sections

The phenomenological cutoff mass $M_{cut}$ can be directly mapped to fundamental particle physics parameters. For a velocity-independent dark matter-baryon scattering cross-section ($n=0$) and a reference dark matter mass of $m_\chi = 1$ GeV, the mapping is approximately $\sigma_0/m_\chi \approx 10^{-29} \times M_{cut}$ [cm$^2$/GeV].

Applying this mapping to the Fisher forecast results yields the projected sensitivity of the 21 cm forest to the scattering cross-section. At $z=7$ with 10 sightlines, the 1-$\sigma$ sensitivity reaches $\sigma_0/m_\chi \approx 1.32 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV. Even at $z=15$, the sensitivity remains highly competitive at $\sim 4.18 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV.

To contextualize these results, we compare the 21 cm forest projections against existing cosmological and astrophysical constraints (visualized in <code>step_4_cross_section_constraints_4_20260413_113759.png</code>):
* **Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB):** Constraints from Planck data (e.g., Xu et al. 2018) limit the cross-section to $\sigma_0/m_\chi < 10^{-22}$ cm$^2$/GeV. The CMB probes linear scales at $z \sim 1100$, before the cumulative effects of collisional damping have fully manifested in the non-linear collapse of small halos.
* **Global 21 cm Signal:** Limits derived from the EDGES and SARAS3 experiments place constraints at the $\sim 10^{-21}$ cm$^2$/GeV level, primarily driven by the thermal cooling channel.
* **Lyman-$\alpha$ Forest:** Current constraints from the Lyman-$\alpha$ forest are on the order of $\sim 10^{-24}$ cm$^2$/GeV. The Lyman-$\alpha$ forest probes larger mass scales (higher temperatures) and lower redshifts ($z \sim 2-5$), where non-linear structure formation has partially erased the primordial suppression signature.

The 21 cm forest is projected to outperform the CMB by four orders of magnitude and the Lyman-$\alpha$ forest by two orders of magnitude. This exceptional sensitivity is a direct consequence of the physical scales probed by the 21 cm forest: it is uniquely sensitive to the lowest-mass minihalos ($10^4 - 10^7 M_\odot$) at high redshifts ($z > 7$), which are the exact structures most vulnerable to collisional damping from dark matter-baryon interactions.

## 5. Optimal Observational Strategy for the SKA Era

Given the observational expense of deep integration times required for 21 cm forest detection with the SKA, optimizing the observational strategy is paramount. This study provides a data-driven blueprint for future surveys by identifying the optimal observational windows in both optical depth ($\tau$) and redshift ($z$).

### 5.1. The Optimal Optical Depth Window
An analysis of the per-$\tau$-bin Fisher information reveals that the constraining power of the 21 cm forest is not uniformly distributed across the absorption spectrum. For instance, at $z=10$, 50% of the total Fisher information regarding $M_{cut}$ is concentrated in the narrow optical depth range of $\tau \in [0.029, 0.153]$, and 80% of the information is contained within $\tau \in [0.022, 0.277]$.

This localized information density has clear physical origins. Absorbers with very low optical depths ($\tau < 0.01$) are predominantly generated by the diffuse IGM and the outskirts of halos, which are less sensitive to the total halo mass cutoff. Conversely, absorbers with very high optical depths ($\tau > 0.3$) correspond to rare, massive halos ($M \gg 10^6 M_\odot$) that remain largely unsuppressed by the moderate $M_{cut}$ values of interest. The intermediate $\tau$ regime corresponds precisely to the core lines of sight through the abundant $10^4 - 10^5 M_\odot$ minihalos that are actively being erased by the dark matter scattering. Consequently, observational pipelines should prioritize high signal-to-noise characterization of intermediate absorbers, rather than expending resources attempting to detect the weakest diffuse lines or waiting for rare, saturated absorption troughs.

### 5.2. The Optimal Redshift Window
Determining the optimal redshift for observations requires balancing absolute signal-to-noise against fractional sensitivity and astrophysical systematics.

From a purely statistical standpoint, the absolute Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) peaks at the lowest redshifts. For a cutoff of $M_{cut} = 10^4 M_\odot$ and 10 sightlines, the SNR is 19.85 at $z=7$, steadily declining to 5.21 at $z=15$. This is driven by the higher baseline abundance of halos at lower redshifts, which minimizes Poisson noise.

However, the *fractional* sensitivity ($\Delta N / N$) exhibits the opposite trend. The relative impact of a $10^4 M_\odot$ cutoff increases from a $47.5\%$ suppression at $z=7$ to a $79.8\%$ suppression at $z=15$. At higher redshifts, the halo mass function is steeper, and a larger fraction of the total halo population resides near the cutoff mass, making the universe more sensitive to small-scale suppression.

Furthermore, observations at $z \sim 7-8$ face severe astrophysical challenges. This epoch corresponds to the late stages of Reionization, where the IGM is highly ionized, and the surviving neutral islands are subject to intense, fluctuating ultraviolet and X-ray backgrounds. These complex astrophysical processes can mimic or obscure the dark matter suppression signature.

Taking these factors into account, we identify $z \sim 10$ as the optimal observational sweet spot (visualized in <code>step_5_optimal_observational_window_5_1776080623.png</code>). At $z=10$, the fractional sensitivity is exceptionally high (a $10^5 M_\odot$ cutoff removes $77\%$ of absorbers), the absolute SNR remains robust (SNR $\approx 20.5$ for $M_{cut}=10^5 M_\odot$), and the universe is largely pre-reionization, providing a cleaner cosmological background free from the severe systematics of patchy ionization.

## 6. Conclusion

This semianalytic study establishes the 21 cm forest as a premier, highly sensitive probe of dark matter-baryon scattering. We have demonstrated that the signal is overwhelmingly dominated by the structure suppression channel, rendering it highly complementary to the thermal-focused global 21 cm signal. By leveraging the spectral shape of the absorber distribution, degeneracies with redshift evolution and overall normalization can be effectively broken. Fisher forecasts indicate that SKA-era observations of just 10 background sightlines at $z \sim 10$ can constrain the scattering cross-section to $\sim 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV, surpassing existing CMB and Lyman-$\alpha$ limits by orders of magnitude. By targeting the optimal optical depth window of $\tau \sim 0.02 - 0.2$ at $z \sim 10$, future radio arrays possess the capability to definitively test velocity-independent and Coulomb-like dark matter-baryon interactions at the threshold of minihalo formation.
