**Hypothesis: The "Differential Redshift-Optical Depth" (DROD) Signature as a Discriminator for DM-Baryon Scattering vs. Astrophysical Nuisance Parameters.**

While previous iterations established that $dN/d\tau$ at $z \sim 10$ is sensitive to $M_{cut}$, the current Fisher analysis assumes a simple normalization shift for astrophysical nuisance parameters. I hypothesize that the *redshift evolution* of the spectral shape of $dN/d\tau$ is fundamentally different for DM-induced structure suppression (which is governed by a fixed $M_{cut}$ transfer function) compared to astrophysical uncertainties like the ionizing background or halo mass function normalization (which evolve with $z$ according to standard reionization physics). 

By constructing a "Redshift-Spectral Gradient" matrix—defined as $\nabla_{z, \tau} = \frac{\partial^2 (dN/d\tau)}{\partial z \partial M_{cut}}$—we can isolate the DM-baryon scattering signal from the astrophysical nuisance parameters. I propose to test if the specific "tilt" in the $\tau$-distribution of absorbers across the $z=7–15$ range acts as a unique fingerprint for $M_{cut}$. This approach will determine if the 21 cm forest can break the degeneracy between DM-baryon scattering and reionization-era astrophysical evolution by analyzing the *rate of change* of the optical depth distribution across multiple redshifts, rather than treating each redshift as an independent measurement. This will provide a more robust constraint that is less susceptible to systematic errors in the assumed halo mass function normalization.
