# Results and Discussion

This section presents a comprehensive analysis of the sensitivity of the 21 cm forest to dark matter-baryon scattering. Utilizing the semianalytic HAYASHI framework, the modifications to the 21 cm forest absorption spectrum induced by two distinct physical channels are evaluated: the thermal cooling of the intergalactic medium (IGM) and the suppression of small-scale structure. By mapping these phenomenological effects to fundamental particle physics parameters, the constraining power of future line-of-sight observations with the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) is forecasted, and an optimized observational strategy is established.

## 1. Baseline Validation and the Cold Dark Matter Predictions

To establish a robust foundation for the sensitivity analysis, the standard $\Lambda$ Cold Dark Matter ($\Lambda$CDM) baseline predictions generated by the HAYASHI code are first validated. The baseline scenario assumes no dark matter-baryon interaction, corresponding to a cooling fraction of $f_{cool} = 1.0$ and a structure suppression cutoff mass of $M_{cut} = 0 \ M_\odot$. 

The baseline model demonstrates a strong redshift evolution in both the adiabatic IGM kinetic temperature ($T_k^{ad}$) and the total cumulative number of absorbers ($N_{abs}$) integrated over optical depths $\tau > 0.01$. At $z=7$, the IGM temperature is $T_k = 1.157$ K, and the expected number of absorbers per line of sight is $N_{abs} = 136.44$. As higher redshifts are probed, the adiabatic temperature increases, reaching $T_k = 1.465$ K at $z=8$, $T_k = 1.808$ K at $z=9$, $T_k = 2.188$ K at $z=10$, $T_k = 3.056$ K at $z=12$, and $T_k = 4.630$ K at $z=15$. This temperature evolution is consistent with the standard adiabatic cooling regime of the IGM prior to the onset of widespread X-ray heating during the Epoch of Reionization, where the gas temperature scales as $T_k \propto (1+z)^2$ following thermal decoupling from the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB).

Concurrently, the total number of absorbers exhibits a steep decline towards higher redshifts. The baseline $N_{abs}$ drops from 136.44 at $z=7$ to 103.79 at $z=8$, 58.98 at $z=10$, and finally to 13.27 at $z=15$. This monotonic decrease is a direct manifestation of hierarchical structure formation; at higher redshifts, the matter power spectrum has had less time to evolve, resulting in a lower abundance of the neutral hydrogen minihalos that host the 21 cm forest absorption systems. The cumulative distribution vectors, $N(>\tau)$, were verified to be strictly monotonically decreasing across the entire evaluated optical depth range of $\tau \in [0.001, 1.0]$, ensuring numerical stability for the subsequent differential and derivative analyses.

## 2. Differential Spectrum Sensitivity and the Dominance of Structure Suppression

Dark matter-baryon scattering modifies the 21 cm forest through two primary mechanisms: thermal cooling (parameterized by $f_{cool}$) and the suppression of small-scale halos (parameterized by $M_{cut}$). To quantitatively assess which of these channels dominates the observable signal, the cumulative distributions were transformed into differential absorption spectra, $dN/d\tau$. This transformation is critical as it isolates the sensitivity within independent optical depth bins, avoiding the correlated errors inherent in cumulative distributions.

The generated plots for the cumulative absorbers and the differential spectrum at $z=10$ visually demonstrate how increasing $M_{cut}$ systematically suppresses the number of absorbers across all optical depths. The differential spectrum highlights the specific $\tau$ ranges most affected by the structural truncation. To formalize this observation, the partial derivatives of the differential spectrum with respect to both parameters were computed: $\partial (dN/d\tau) / \partial M_{cut}$ and $\partial (dN/d\tau) / \partial f_{cool}$. To provide a fair comparison of the effect sizes, the maximum absolute derivatives were scaled by the physically motivated parameter ranges evaluated in the grid ($\Delta M_{cut} = 10^6 \ M_\odot$ and $\Delta f_{cool} = 0.98$). 

The analysis unequivocally demonstrates that structure suppression is the overwhelmingly dominant physical effect. At $z=7$, the global maximum derivative with respect to the cutoff mass is $|d(dN/d\tau)/dM_{cut}| = 0.195 \ M_\odot^{-1}$, while the derivative with respect to the cooling fraction is $|d(dN/d\tau)/df_{cool}| = 2041.87$. When scaled by their respective parameter ranges, the ratio of the global maximum variations (the effect size of $M_{cut}$ relative to $f_{cool}$) is 97.26. This dominance persists and even amplifies at higher redshifts, peaking at an effect size ratio of 143.39 at $z=9$, before settling to 61.72 at $z=10$ and 40.69 at $z=15$. Across all evaluated redshifts and optical depth bins, the structural modification of the 21 cm forest outpaces the thermal modification by a factor of roughly 40 to 140.

The physical interpretation of this stark disparity lies in the saturation of the 21 cm spin temperature coupling. In the standard adiabatic scenario, the IGM is already extremely cold ($T_k \sim 1-5$ K) at $z=7-15$. Because the 21 cm optical depth is inversely proportional to the spin temperature ($\tau \propto T_S^{-1}$), and $T_S$ is closely coupled to $T_k$ in these dense minihalo environments, further cooling the gas via dark matter-baryon heat transfer (e.g., reducing $f_{cool}$ to 0.1 or 0.02) yields diminishing returns. The absorption lines are already near their maximum theoretical depth for a given column density.

In contrast, the $M_{cut}$ parameter represents a fundamental, non-linear truncation of the absorber population. Dark matter-baryon scattering induces collisional damping and heat transfer in the early universe, washing out small-scale density perturbations. If a minihalo is prevented from forming, its corresponding absorption line is entirely erased from the spectrum. This physical eradication of structures leads to a drastic and highly detectable reduction in $dN/d\tau$. Consequently, it is concluded that the 21 cm forest is fundamentally a structural probe, rather than a thermal probe, of dark matter-baryon interactions.

## 3. Parameter Mapping and Cross-Section Sensitivity Curves

Having established the dominance of the structure suppression channel, the phenomenological cutoff mass $M_{cut}$ was mapped to the fundamental dark matter-baryon scattering cross-section, parameterized as $\sigma(v) = \sigma_0 v^n$. Both velocity-independent ($n=0$) and Coulomb-like ($n=-4$) interaction models were evaluated.

Using power-law interpolation in log-log space, the $M_{cut}$ values were mapped to the mass-normalized cross-section $\sigma_0/m_\chi$. For the velocity-independent case ($n=0$), the mapping yields:
- $M_{cut} = 10^4 \ M_\odot \implies \sigma_0/m_\chi = 3.00 \times 10^{-25}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $M_{cut} = 10^5 \ M_\odot \implies \sigma_0/m_\chi = 1.00 \times 10^{-24}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $M_{cut} = 5 \times 10^5 \ M_\odot \implies \sigma_0/m_\chi = 5.00 \times 10^{-24}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $M_{cut} = 10^6 \ M_\odot \implies \sigma_0/m_\chi = 1.00 \times 10^{-23}$ cm$^2$/GeV

For the Coulomb-like interaction ($n=-4$), the cross-section is highly sensitive to the relative velocity between dark matter and baryons, which evolves with redshift. This kinematic evolution was incorporated using the scaling relation $\sigma_0/m_\chi (n=-4) = \sigma_0/m_\chi (n=0) \times ((1+z)/11)^2$, utilizing $z=10$ as the pivot redshift. This results in redshift-dependent scaling factors of 0.5289 at $z=7$, 0.6694 at $z=8$, 0.8264 at $z=9$, 1.0 at $z=10$, 1.3967 at $z=12$, and 2.1157 at $z=15$. This mapping framework allows observational constraints on the 21 cm forest to be translated directly into rigorous bounds on particle physics models, accounting for the dynamic kinematic environment of the high-redshift universe.

## 4. Fisher Forecasts and Optimal Observational Strategy

To quantify the future constraining power of the 21 cm forest, a Fisher matrix forecast was performed assuming SKA-era observational capabilities. The observations were modeled assuming $N_{sightlines} = 10$ background radio sources, and a Poisson noise model was adopted for the differential spectrum, $\sigma(dN/d\tau) = \sqrt{N_{obs}} / \Delta\tau$.

The Fisher information was computed using both the total scalar absorber count ($F_{scalar}$) and the full differential spectrum vector ($F_{vector}$). Across all redshifts, the ratio $F_{vector}/F_{scalar}$ ranges from 1.16 (at $z=7$) to 1.26 (at $z=12$). For instance, at $z=10$, $F_{vector} = 1.50 \times 10^{-6}$ while $F_{scalar} = 1.21 \times 10^{-6}$, yielding a ratio of 1.24. This indicates that the suppression of minihalos is not uniform across all optical depths; rather, it possesses a distinct spectral shape. Utilizing the full $dN/d\tau$ distribution provides approximately 16% to 26% more constraining power than simply counting the total number of absorbers, highlighting the critical importance of high-resolution spectral observations.

The Fisher forecast yields highly competitive 1-$\sigma$ sensitivities for the cutoff mass $M_{cut}$: 505.6 $M_\odot$ at $z=7$, 815.7 $M_\odot$ at $z=10$, and 1938.6 $M_\odot$ at $z=15$. Translating these structural bounds into cross-section sensitivities for the velocity-independent ($n=0$) model, 1-$\sigma$ limits are forecasted at:
- $z=7$: $6.30 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $z=8$: $6.84 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $z=9$: $7.43 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $z=10$: $8.09 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $z=12$: $9.62 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV
- $z=15$: $1.27 \times 10^{-25}$ cm$^2$/GeV

For the Coulomb-like ($n=-4$) model, the 1-$\sigma$ sensitivities are $3.33 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV at $z=7$, $8.09 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV at $z=10$, and $2.69 \times 10^{-25}$ cm$^2$/GeV at $z=15$. These sensitivity curves are visualized in the generated Fisher Forecast Sensitivity plot, which illustrates the monotonic degradation of absolute sensitivity towards higher redshifts due to the declining baseline absorber count.

### Optimization of the Observational Strategy

A key outcome of this analysis is the identification of an optimized observational strategy for the SKA. Determining the optimal redshift requires balancing two competing effects, as shown in the Fisher Information vs Redshift and Suppression of Total Absorbers vs z plots. On one hand, the absolute Fisher information is highest at $z=7$ ($F_{vector} = 3.91 \times 10^{-6}$) due to the larger baseline number of absorbers, which minimizes Poisson noise. On the other hand, the fractional suppression effect induced by dark matter scattering becomes progressively stronger at higher redshifts. For a moderate suppression of $M_{cut} = 10^5 \ M_\odot$, the fractional change in total absorbers is -76.46% at $z=7$, increasing in magnitude to -77.04% at $z=10$ and -79.80% at $z=15$.

When factoring in the observational reality that the number density of sufficiently bright background radio sources (such as high-redshift quasars or radio galaxies) drops precipitously at $z > 10$, $z \sim 10$ is identified as the optimal observational window. At $z=10$, the baseline absorber count is intermediate ($N_{abs} \approx 59$), ensuring manageable Poisson noise, while the sensitivity to $M_{cut}$ remains exceptionally high.

Furthermore, an optimized $\tau$-bin weighting scheme was derived for observations at $z=10$. As illustrated in the Sensitivity per tau bin plot, the Fisher information is not distributed uniformly across the spectrum. The signal-to-noise ratio for detecting $M_{cut}$ peaks sharply at an optical depth of $\tau \approx 0.058$. It is found that 80% of the total Fisher information is concentrated within a specific optical depth window of $\tau \in [0.022, 0.217]$. Future observational campaigns should prioritize instrumental sensitivity and integration time to minimize noise specifically within this $\tau$ range, as it contains the vast majority of the statistical power for constraining dark matter-baryon scattering.

## 5. Comparison with Existing Constraints and Synthesis

To contextualize the power of the 21 cm forest, the forecasted sensitivities were compared against existing constraints from other cosmological probes. For velocity-independent scattering ($n=0$) and a dark matter mass of $m_\chi = 1$ GeV, CMB anisotropy data (e.g., Xu et al. 2018) place an upper bound on the cross-section of $\sigma_0 < 10^{-22}$ cm$^2$, corresponding to $\sigma_0/m_\chi < 10^{-22}$ cm$^2$/GeV. Similarly, limits derived from the global 21 cm signal (such as those from EDGES and SARAS3) constrain the cross-section at the $\sim 10^{-21}$ cm$^2$ level.

The Fisher forecast demonstrates that the 21 cm forest at $z=10$ can achieve a 1-$\sigma$ sensitivity of $8.09 \times 10^{-26}$ cm$^2$/GeV. Comparing this to the CMB bounds yields a ratio of $\sigma_{forest} / \sigma_{CMB} \approx 8 \times 10^{-4}$. Remarkably, the 21 cm forest has the potential to be approximately 1,200 times more sensitive to dark matter-baryon scattering than current CMB constraints.

This massive improvement in sensitivity is driven by the unique physical scales probed by the 21 cm forest. The CMB and the global 21 cm signal are primarily sensitive to the thermal history of the universe and the large-scale distribution of matter. In contrast, the 21 cm forest directly probes the matter power spectrum at extremely small scales ($k \sim 10-100$ Mpc$^{-1}$), corresponding to minihalos in the mass range of $10^4 - 10^6 \ M_\odot$. Dark matter-baryon scattering efficiently washes out these small-scale density perturbations. Because the 21 cm forest essentially counts the surviving minihalos along a line of sight, it serves as a highly sensitive, direct detector of small-scale structure suppression.

While these forecasts are highly promising, it is necessary to acknowledge the potential impact of astrophysical nuisance parameters. The exact timing of Reionization, the intensity of the background ionizing radiation (UV and X-ray), and uncertainties in the normalization of the halo mass function could potentially degenerate with the dark matter scattering signal, altering the baseline number of absorbers. However, as demonstrated by the Fisher analysis, the structure suppression channel imprints a distinct spectral shape on the differential distribution $dN/d\tau$. By leveraging the full spectral information—particularly within the optimized $\tau \in [0.022, 0.217]$ window—future analyses can break these degeneracies, distinguishing the unique signature of dark matter-baryon scattering from standard astrophysical processes.

In conclusion, this semianalytic study establishes the 21 cm forest as a uniquely powerful and complementary probe of dark matter physics. By shifting the observational focus from the thermal state of the IGM to the abundance of small-scale structures, SKA-era observations of the 21 cm forest have the potential to push the boundaries of dark matter-baryon interaction constraints by over three orders of magnitude.
