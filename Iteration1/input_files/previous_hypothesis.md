The current analysis assumes a universal structure suppression model $T(M) = (1 + (M_{cut}/M)^{1.5})^{-2}$, which treats all minihalos as equally susceptible to DM-baryon scattering. However, the 21 cm forest is sensitive to both host halos and subhalos, which occupy different environments and have different density profiles. I hypothesize that the "spectral shape" of the suppression in the $dN/d\tau$ distribution is not just a function of $M_{cut}$, but is fundamentally degenerate with the *spatial distribution* of subhalos within host halos. By decomposing the HAYASHI output into "host-only" vs. "host+subhalo" contributions, we can identify a "subhalo-suppression signature"—a specific distortion in the high-$\tau$ tail of the distribution that is distinct from the global suppression of host halos. I propose to test if the ratio of high-$\tau$ to low-$\tau$ absorbers acts as a "standard candle" for DM-baryon scattering that is robust against astrophysical uncertainties (like the ionizing background normalization), which typically shift the entire $N(>\tau)$ curve but preserve the relative subhalo-to-host ratio. This will provide a more resilient constraint on $M_{cut}$ that is less susceptible to the nuisance parameters identified in the previous iteration.
